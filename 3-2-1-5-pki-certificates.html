3.2.1 Certificate Authorities

üèõÔ∏è Certificate Authorities ‚Äì The ID Offices of the Internet

A Certificate Authority (CA) is a trusted organization whose job is to:

  • Verify identities (individuals, organizations, domains).
  • Issue and sign certificates linking identities to public keys.
  • Publish and manage those certificates and revocation information.[web:173][web:176][web:185]

Your browser and OS come with a built‚Äëin list of trusted root CA certificates. If a website‚Äôs certificate chains back to one of those, the browser shows a padlock and treats it as trusted.[web:176][web:182][web:185]

High‚ÄëLevel Web PKI Story
1) CA has its own key pair and root certificate (self-signed).
2) Website generates key pair and CSR with its public key.
3) CA verifies the website's identity (domain, organization).
4) CA signs the website's public key and info -> site certificate.
5) Browser connects to site, receives its certificate.
6) Browser checks signature against trusted CA roots/intermediates.
7) If valid, browser shows "secure" and uses the public key for TLS.[web:173][web:176][web:185]
üè¢ Private CAs vs Public CAs (Exam‚Äëstyle) ‚ñ∂
  • Private / internal CA ‚Äì run by a company for its own users/devices (for example, internal VPN, Wi‚ÄëFi, code). Trusted only inside that organization‚Äôs environment.
  • Public / third‚Äëparty CA ‚Äì commercial CA trusted by many browsers and OSes (DigiCert, Let‚Äôs Encrypt, etc.). Used for public‚Äëfacing websites and B2B services.[web:176][web:185]

On the exam, ‚Äúinternal services only, not accessible from internet‚Äù ‚Üí likely use a private CA. ‚ÄúPublic website that must be trusted by customers worldwide‚Äù ‚Üí use a public CA.

3.2.2 Digital Certificates

üìú Digital Certificates ‚Äì ID Cards for Keys

A digital certificate (usually X.509 format) is like an ID card that says:

  • ‚ÄúThis public key belongs to this subject (website/user/device).‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúThis CA vouches for that statement.‚Äù

The certificate is digitally signed by the issuing CA, so tampering or fake certificates can be detected.[web:173][web:176][web:179][web:182]

X.509 certificate üìú ‚Äì standard format for certificates used on the web and in PKI.[web:173][web:176][web:179][web:182]
Typical Certificate Fields
Subject            -> Who the cert is for (e.g., www.example.com, or a user)[web:176][web:179]

Issuer             -> The CA that issued the certificate[web:176][web:173]

A certificate does not contain the private key. The private key stays on the owner‚Äôs system; the certificate only holds the public key plus identity info and the CA‚Äôs signature.[web:176][web:179]
3.2.3 Root of Trust

üå≥ Root of Trust & Chain of Trust

PKI trust works like a family tree. At the top is a root CA that everyone explicitly trusts. Below that are intermediate CAs, and at the bottom are leaf certificates (websites, users, devices).[web:173][web:176][web:182]

Hierarchy Model
        [ Root CA ]   (self-signed, stored in trust store)
             |
       ----------------
       |              |
 [Intermediate CA1]  [Intermediate CA2]
       |                 |
   [www.example.com]  [vpn.company.com]

If you trust Root CA, you can trust all certs with a valid chain down from it.[web:173][web:176][web:182]

Browsers and OSes ship with many root CA certificates preinstalled. That list is the user‚Äôs root of trust.[web:176][web:182][web:185]

üß± Single CA vs Hierarchical PKI ‚ñ∂
  • Single CA ‚Äì one CA directly issues all end‚Äëentity certificates. Simple, often used internally. But if that CA is compromised, the entire PKI collapses.
  • Hierarchical model ‚Äì root CA only signs intermediates; intermediates sign leaf certs. Root can be kept offline; compromise of one intermediate doesn‚Äôt destroy the whole tree.[web:173][web:176][web:182]

This chain from leaf ‚Üí intermediate ‚Üí root is called the certificate chain or chain of trust. Verifying that chain is a key step in TLS handshakes.

üßæ Self‚ÄëSigned Certificates (Dev or Danger?) ‚ñ∂

A self‚Äësigned certificate is signed by the same entity it‚Äôs issued to (subject = issuer). Useful for:

  • Lab environments

But because no trusted CA vouches for it, browsers mark it as ‚Äúuntrusted.‚Äù You can manually accept it, but self‚Äësigned certs should not protect critical public services.

3.2.4 Certificate Signing Requests

üì® CSRs ‚Äì Asking a CA for a Certificate

To get a certificate, an applicant generates a key pair locally, then creates a Certificate Signing Request (CSR) containing the public key and identity info, and sends it to the CA.[web:174][web:177][web:180][web:186]

CSR Contents (PKCS#10 style)
- Subject info (DN): CN, O, OU, L, ST, C[web:174][web:180]
- Public key (to be certified)[web:174][web:176]
- Requested extensions (e.g., SAN names)[web:174][web:176][web:186]
- Self-signature with private key (proves you control the private key)[web:174][web:177]
Story: Applying for a Digital Passport
You fill out a form with your name, address, and photo (identity), and include a way for them to contact you. Then you sign the form. The government office checks the info, confirms you are really you, and then prints and signs your passport.

Similarly, a CSR is like that form; the CA reviews it, verifies you‚Äôre allowed to use that domain or identity, and then issues a signed certificate.

3.2.5 Subject Name & SAN

üåê CN, SAN & Wildcards ‚Äì Which Names Does the Cert Cover?

Certificates need to state which hostnames or identities they cover. Historically, the Common Name (CN) field was used for the main hostname, but modern best practice is to put hostnames in the Subject Alternative Name (SAN) extension.[web:176][web:179][web:185]

CN (Common Name) ‚Äì older way to store the main name (for example, www.example.com).
SAN (Subject Alternative Name) ‚Äì extension that can list multiple DNS names, IPs, or even emails.[web:175][web:178][web:176][web:187]
SAN Examples
DNS Names in SAN:
  - www.example.com
  - example.com
  - api.example.com
  - members.example.org[web:175][web:178][web:181]

Wildcard example:
  - *.example.com  -> covers a.example.com, api.example.com, login.example.com, etc.[web:175][web:178][web:187]

Browsers now primarily check the SAN list to see if the certificate matches the hostname you typed in the URL.[web:176][web:185]

‚≠ê Wildcard vs Explicit SAN Entries ‚ñ∂
  • Wildcard cert (for example, *.example.com) ‚Äì one certificate can cover many first‚Äëlevel subdomains like app.example.com, mail.example.com.[web:178][web:187]
  • SAN cert ‚Äì lists specific hostnames and/or wildcard names in SAN (for example, www.example.com, api.example.net, *.dev.example.com). [web:175][web:178][web:181][web:184]

On the exam, if they mention protecting multiple different FQDNs on one cert, think SAN. If they mention covering any subdomain under one domain, think wildcard.
